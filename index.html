The Impact of Tariffs on U.S. Data Centers

Exploring how tariffs and energy demand are reshaping the future of AI infrastructure.

The United States leads the world in data centers, with more than all other countries combined and ten times more than any other single country. This dominance is critical as AI-driven demand for data centers continues to grow.

Energy demand from U.S. data centers is forecast to quadruple by 2030 due to the rapid adoption of AI technologies. This growth will require unprecedented electricity production.

Building data centers involves sourcing materials like steel, aluminum, and copper—many of which are heavily impacted by U.S. tariffs. These tariffs significantly increase construction costs.

For example, U.S. imports of steel face a 25% tariff under Section 232, while aluminum imports from Canada and Mexico are exempt if USMCA-compliant. Copper tariffs remain uncertain but could drive prices higher.

Major companies like Apple and Amazon have pledged billions toward expanding AI-focused data centers despite rising costs. Apple's $500 billion commitment includes advanced manufacturing facilities in Texas and Arizona.

“Instability benefits nobody,” says Prof. Susan Ariel Aaronson, highlighting the contradictions in U.S. policies impacting AI competitiveness.
