Tariffs Threaten U.S. Dominance in AI Infrastructure

New trade policies create costly uncertainties for data center construction

Last week, President Donald Trump announced sweeping tariffs on steel, aluminum, and copper imports—materials critical to data center construction. The United States operates more data centers than all other countries combined—ten times more than any single nation. This infrastructure forms the backbone of America's AI ambitions, but these tariffs threaten to derail progress.

Energy consumption by U.S. data centers is expected to quadruple by 2030 as AI adoption accelerates. Meeting this demand requires massive infrastructure expansion, which is now complicated by rising costs due to tariffs.

Building these facilities requires steel, aluminum, and copper—materials now subject to complex tariff regimes:

  • Steel: Subject to a 25% tariff under Section 232 since March.
  • Aluminum: Tariff increased to 25%, with exemptions for USMCA-compliant imports.
  • Copper: Potential tariffs under investigation, capped at a possible 25% rate.
"Instability benefits nobody," says Prof. Susan Ariel Aaronson of George Washington University.

Major tech companies like Apple and Amazon have pledged billions toward expanding AI-focused data centers despite rising costs. Apple alone has committed $500 billion over four years to build advanced facilities in Texas and Arizona.

This investment reflects the growing importance of AI infrastructure as companies race to meet surging demand while navigating policy contradictions that could undermine U.S. competitiveness.
